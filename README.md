# How Does the World Evolve? A New Theory That Unifies Intelligence and Risk

Why does an AI model suddenly become intelligent? Why do financial markets suddenly crash? These seemingly completely different phenomena may share the same underlying law.

A newly proposed theory called "Evolution Theory" attempts to explain these phenomena with a unified language. This article uses the simplest words to tell you what this theory is about.

## The Starting Point: A Strange Triangle

Imagine a right triangle with two legs of length 1 and i. Here i is the imaginary unit, whose square equals -1. According to the Pythagorean theorem, the square of the hypotenuse equals 1² + i² = 1 + (-1) = 0, so the hypotenuse is 0.

This result is counterintuitive—two solid legs adding up to zero. But this is precisely the seed of the entire theory.

Think of the real number 1 as "order"—patterns, structures, information. Think of the imaginary number i as "disorder"—noise, randomness, uncertainty. When order and disorder completely cancel out, the system is in equilibrium, invisible. When order dominates, the system becomes increasingly ordered; when disorder dominates, the system becomes increasingly chaotic.

This is the essence of evolution: **the ebb and flow of order and disorder**.

## Two Worlds: Intelligence and Risk

Evolution Theory divides systems into two types:

**Positive-order systems**: processes where order continuously increases. For example, an AI model becoming smarter during training, a child gaining knowledge through learning, a species becoming more complex through evolution. This process is called "ordering."

**Negative-order systems**: processes where disorder continuously increases. For example, financial markets becoming increasingly unstable before a crash, an ecosystem becoming more fragile before collapse, a society becoming more divided before unrest. This process is called "risk."

These two processes appear completely opposite, but they follow the same laws—just in opposite directions, like looking in a mirror.

## Three Invisible Players

In any system, three forces are competing behind the scenes:

1. **External ordered input**: good things coming from outside. For AI, it's training data; for financial markets, it's policies and regulations; for learning, it's knowledge from teachers.

2. **External disordered input**: bad things coming from outside. For AI, it's data noise; for financial markets, it's unexpected news; for learning, it's misinformation.

3. **Internal game**: the system's inherent chaos. AI parameters compete with each other; investors play games with each other; neurons in the brain compete with each other.

The key point is: **internal games themselves only produce disorder**. To get order, you must absorb good things from outside. But absorbing good things itself intensifies internal games—that's why learning new things can sometimes be confusing, and new policies can cause short-term market fluctuations.

## Critical Points: From Quantitative to Qualitative Change

Have you noticed that many things happen "suddenly"?

An AI model trains for a long time without progress, then one day it suddenly works; markets are stable for a long time, then one day they crash; you practice a skill with no improvement, then one day you master it.

This is the "critical point"—quantitative accumulation reaching a threshold triggers qualitative change.

Evolution Theory reveals that different systems approach critical points in different ways:

- Systems that get better (positive-order) usually transition smoothly, like a student gradually improving. You can tell when improvement will slow down by watching the "inflection point of progress speed."

- Systems that get worse (negative-order) usually erupt suddenly, like market crashes. But there are warnings before the eruption—fluctuations get larger and larger, like small earthquakes before a major one. You can detect danger by watching "volatility."

## Two Examples: Finance and AI

**Financial markets**: By measuring investor sentiment as an index and calculating its degree of chaos, researchers found that in internally-driven markets (like the Hang Seng Index), peaks in chaos can warn of risks in advance (p=0.0342). In markets dominated by external news (like US stocks), the warning effect is less obvious. But through signal separation techniques, hidden internal game signals can still be extracted even from US stocks.

**Neural networks**: When training a model to recognize fashion images, researchers found that the intensity of parameter competition (measured by gradient fluctuations) peaked 18 epochs before overfitting occurred (p=0.0292). They also discovered that near the training critical point, applying periodic fluctuations to the learning rate at a specific frequency amplified the model's performance fluctuations by more than 9 times—like pushing a swing at just the right rhythm makes it go higher. Additionally, during training plateaus, adding appropriate amounts of noise actually allowed the model to continue improving.

## Implications for AI's Future

This theory tells us that true intelligence isn't about endlessly piling on data and computation—it's about learning to "ride the game."

One particularly interesting implication is: once a model learns how to learn, it may no longer need to rely on backpropagation; it can evolve through continuous interaction between memory and input. This is a possible path toward artificial general intelligence—memory-driven self-evolution.

This sounds a bit like science fiction, but this is exactly how humans learn. We don't need to restructure our brains every time we learn something new; we simply integrate new information with existing knowledge.

## Why This Theory Matters

This is the first theory that unifies the emergence of intelligence and the outbreak of risk. Previously, AI researchers and financial researchers spoke different languages; now they can communicate with the same vocabulary.

Finally, this theory reminds us: **order and disorder are two sides of the same coin**. To create, you must learn to embrace chaos; to be stable, you must know how to release pressure. Perhaps the wisdom of evolution lies in this simple triangle.
